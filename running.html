Chapter Five

Running the Other Side of Normal

You're not the architect of a horror story. You're not the hunter. You're the whole city.

Running TOSON isn't the same as running most games. You're not the architect of a horror story. You're not the hunter. You're not an adversary working against the players, and you're not the author guiding them toward a conclusion you've already written.

You're a neighbor. You're the whole city: the Folk community with its politics and its grief, the Watch grinding through its procedures, the hunters who believe what they believe, the ordinary human world that doesn't know any of it exists. Your job is to make that world feel real: consequential, alive, and complicated. Not to threaten the players, but to give them something worth navigating.

That means you build situations, not stories. A Marked character needs a trial but has no Sponsor willing to back them. A Balance circle has started asking quiet questions about a Folk neighborhood and nobody knows yet. Where those situations go is not your business. That's theirs.

What Is Not Your Job

Running the players through a plot you've already written. Deciding in advance what the Peace costs them. Playing hunters as invincible so the world feels dangerous. Introducing an NPC who fixes what the players broke. The Folk world already has enough pressure in it. Your job is to keep the heat on, not to turn it up so high nobody can move.

Session Zero

Session zero in TOSON is Pathways. You don't need a separate planning session. Character creation is the planning session. Pathways builds your characters and your shared world at the same time, and by the end of it you'll have a mind map full of hooks ready to use.

Before Pathways begins, have the safety conversation. You've already read about the tools — Lines & Veils, the X-Card, the Open Door — but session zero is when you actually use them. Talk about what themes the group wants to explore, what should stay offscreen, and what the tone should be. TOSON supports a wide range (political drama, horror, quiet character study) but your group needs to agree on which direction before you start building characters who fit that story.

In play-by-post, run Pathways in a dedicated thread or channel. Have everyone post their stage responses publicly so the group can read along and connect to each other's elements. When a player writes down a Relationship, ask who it's with. When they add an element to the mind map, ask what it looks like. Don't let answers stay vague. "A hunter who knows too much" becomes "a former Watch investigator named Reyes who got pushed out after the Alderman case."

Your job during Pathways is to ask questions. Every element a player adds is a hook you can use. Every Relationship they write is something you can lean on. Take notes. You're building your first session while they're building their characters.

Session One

Your job in the first session is not to tell a story. It's to make the world feel real and put pressure on the characters until something interesting happens. Don't plan where the session is going. Play to find out.

Start by making the hidden world present from the first scene. Every location should have a Folk layer: the coffee shop where the owner gives a slight nod to Folk who come in, the police precinct where one detective is on the Watch's informal payroll, the neighborhood that looks ordinary until you know what to look for. Don't ease players in. The world they live in is already there. Show it.

Springboard off Pathways. You have a mind map full of elements the players gave you. Use them immediately. The Relationship someone wrote down? Put that person in the first scene. The element someone added about a Watch investigation? Make it relevant today. The players told you what they care about during character creation. Trust that and build toward it.

In play-by-post, frame scenes with multiple characters early. Solo scenes are fine later, but the first session is not the time for a character to spend three posts alone in their apartment. Put two characters in the same place with conflicting interests and ask what they do. The Relationships built during Pathways make this easy — there's already history between these characters before play begins.

Push on Relationships throughout. In TOSON, Relationships aren't just flavor. They're dice. When a Relationship comes up in play, make it matter. Ask what it costs to call in a favor. Ask what a character owes the person they're leaning on.

The Scene Pool

TOSON uses a modified opposition pool called the Scene Pool. Pools run from a minimum of 3d6 to a maximum of 6d12. The number of dice reflects how long or complex the opposition is. The size of the dice reflects how dangerous each element is.

The GM's Opening Post

Every scene begins with a GM post that establishes the scene and posts the Scene Pool. Describe the setting, the situation, who's present, and what the stakes feel like. Post the pool clearly.

Example Opening Post

The lobby of the Vanguard Global Security regional office is exactly what you'd expect: clean lines, corporate art, a receptionist who looks up from her desk with the particular alertness of someone trained to notice things. The building's security badge system is between you and the server room.

Scene Pool: d8 d8 d6 Scale d8

Players post their actions immediately. They do not need to wait for the GM to roll opposition.

The Player's Action Post

A player posts their character's action, assembles their dice pool, and rolls it. They must reveal their total and effect die before the Scene Pool is rolled. They state which Scene Pool die they're targeting with their effect die.

The GM's Response Post

After a player posts, the GM narrates the consequence, adjusts the Scene Pool, and posts the updated pool. The GM may also spend from the pool to make a direct move. Every GM post ends with the current Scene Pool clearly stated so the next player knows what they're rolling against.

In a live game, GMs can spend a die to add an extra result to their opposition total reactively, after seeing a player's roll. That timing doesn't work in play-by-post. As a replacement: if the GM wants a single exchange to hit harder, they may spend a Scene Pool die in their response post and declare the pool Pressed. On the next player roll only, the Scene Pool keeps three dice in its total instead of two. Players can see this before they roll and factor it into their approach.

The Opposition Principle

Almost every scene in TOSON has meaningful opposition. Opposition is not always a person trying to hurt you. Opposition is anything that creates meaningful stakes and consequences for failure.

Situation What the Opposition Represents
A Marked character attending a public event Exposure, social scrutiny, the possibility that a Society operative is in the room
A Mage negotiating with a Watch council member for a Sponsor The council member's own agenda, their fear of political exposure, and time pressure
A Revenant keeping their corpse's deterioration hidden at a human gathering Observation and the social cost of being seen struggling

When building a Scene Pool, ask what the situation is working against the players. Translate that into dice. The only scene type with no opposition is the tag scene. Everything else should have something pushing back.

GMCs

GMCs in TOSON come in several forms, each suited to a different dramatic role. The Scene Pool handles opposition difficulty. You don't need to track separate hit dice for any GMC. What you do need depends on the type.

Extras

An Extra is the simplest GMC in the game: one trait, nothing more. That trait can represent anything notable about the character — a skill, a piece of equipment, a quality, a connection. Investigator d10. Combat Training d8. Loyal to a Fault d6. Whatever makes this person matter in the scene.

When an Extra is relevant to what's happening, their trait goes into the Scene Pool. When they're helping a player character, they lend their trait die instead. They don't need names, backstory, or elaborate stat blocks. Just the one thing that makes them worth noting.

Features

A Feature is any character important enough to need more than one trait — someone the story returns to or whose presence defines a major scene. They have no distinctions, but carry multiple traits (typically three to six) and can also have SFX, usually two or three, representing the specific ways their power or expertise shapes events.

What makes someone a Feature isn't their alignment or their power level. It's their narrative weight.

Mobs

A Mob is a group treated as a single GMC — a hunter patrol, a crowd that's started paying attention to the wrong thing. Whatever the numbers, they're handled as one. Like a Feature, a Mob carries multiple traits that define its capabilities, but no SFX.

When a Mob enters a scene, their traits contribute to the Scene Pool alongside everything else pressing against the players. As the pool clears, the mob shrinks with it. No separate tracking required. The GM describes what that looks like: stragglers breaking off, the group losing cohesion.

Example Mob Traits

Watch enforcement team: Authorized to Act d8 · Knows These Streets d6

Balance circle on a correction mission: Ideological Conviction d10 · Numbers on Their Side d8

Factions

A Faction is any organization whose institutional weight can be felt in a scene. Not a character — a system. The Watch reaching into a neighborhood through its administrative channels. A Balance circle whose membership spans several professions and knows everyone worth knowing. Like Features, Factions carry multiple traits and can have SFX.

Factions don't need to be hostile to matter. The Watch appearing in a scene might be bureaucratic, protective, or quietly threatening — depending entirely on context and who got drawn in the council lottery. A Balance circle acting on incomplete information is dangerous because they're sincere. The Society is most dangerous when they're simply there, patient, building a file.

Hunters

Hunters being present in a scene isn't automatically a threat. Hunters who are following the rules of the Peace are just people — professionals, believers, family members carrying old knowledge. A Folk character can share a room with a Society operative without the scene becoming dangerous. What matters is whether the hunters are hostile, and what's driving that hostility.

The Watch List is one factor. A Condemned character draws active hunters who feel fully authorized to act. A Marked character draws hunters who are watching, testing, looking for an excuse. An unlisted Folk character draws nothing beyond background wariness — unless something in the scene changes that.

Escalating the Hunter Pool

A useful way to think about escalation is as a series of step-ups. A minimum pool of 3d6 represents low threat — a small number of inexperienced or cautious hunters. Each step up swaps one die for the next size: 3d6 becomes 2d6 1d8, then 1d6 2d8, then 3d8. Keep stepping as the situation warrants. A full Society team hunting a Condemned Folk might sit at 3d10 or higher before the scene even begins.

There is no fixed formula. The Watch List, the hunters' skill, their numbers, and the specific situation all feed into the pool. Build it to reflect what the scene actually feels like.

Locations

A Location is a place with enough presence to affect what happens in it. Locations have distinctions representing what defines them as a space, and a small number of traits covering specific features or dangers. Narrow Stairwell d8. Security Cameras d6. That's often enough.

Unlike every other GMC type, Location distinctions can be used by anyone in the scene. The GM can add them to the Scene Pool — the space working against the players. A player who finds a way to use the environment to their advantage can add a Location distinction to their own pool instead. The same narrow stairwell that makes fighting harder also makes it easier to hold a chokepoint.

Example Locations

Folk community center: Magically Warded d8 · Everyone Knows Everyone d6

Vanguard regional office: Corporate Security d8 · Monitored at All Times d6

Running the Hidden World

The hidden world creates its own pressure. Add when it counts. A Marked character walking through their daily life already has a story. Players will generate their own drama through their choices and relationships. The GM doesn't need to manufacture confrontations the fiction isn't calling for — but when a scene feels flat or the pressure has been absent too long, that's the GM's cue. Drop in a consequence. Escalate a complication. Have someone notice something they shouldn't.

The Watch is slow and political, not fast and decisive. Trials take time. Sponsors are hard to find. Council draws are random. The Watch is not a cavalry — it's a bureaucracy with teeth. Players who expect rescue from the Watch will be disappointed. Players who learn to work the system will find it useful.

The Society and the Balance are not the same threat. The Society is visible — old money, corporate presence, hunters with equipment and grudges. Players can see them coming. The Balance is invisible — ordinary people with extraordinary patience. A Balance circle doesn't announce itself. By the time players realize they're being watched, the watching has been going on for a while.

The Hollow is a resource and a vulnerability. Information on the Hollow travels fast. A Marked character's status update reaches every Folk in the city before the end of the day. The players have access to this information too, but so does everyone watching them.

Hunter Organization Framework

Hunter organizations in TOSON are Factions. When building one:

Traits should reflect what the organization brings institutionally — resources, training, reach, secrecy, ideology. Not what any single hunter can do. Decades of Accumulated Intelligence d10. Global Operative Network d8. Legally Untouchable d8.

SFX should represent what the organization does that no individual can do — bringing institutional weight to bear, activating resources, escalating. The Society's SFX might let them call in a trained team mid-scene. The Balance's SFX might let them invoke their network of ordinarily-invisible members.

Scale die represents reach. A local Balance circle runs at d6. A regional chapter at d8. The Van Helsing Society globally operates at d12.

Attitude matters for how the Faction enters scenes. The Society is hostile. The Balance is neutral-to-hostile depending on context. Build GM guidance around when and why they appear — not just what they can do when they arrive.

For individual hunter GMCs within an organization, use Extra, Mob, or Feature depending on narrative weight. The Faction represents the organization as a whole; individual hunters are their own GMC type.

Building Custom Folk Types

The six Folk Types in this supplement cover a lot of ground, but they don't cover everything. If your table wants to play a Skinwalker, a Kitsune, a Djinn, or something entirely invented — here's how to build one that holds together mechanically and feels right at the table.

The goal is always the same: what does playing this Folk Type feel like? Start there and build toward it.

Step 1

Name the Core Fantasy

Before anything else, identify the central experience of this Folk Type. Power over others? Hunger that can't be fully controlled? Transformation? Ancient age pressing down on a modern life? That experience is what the SFX exist to serve. If you build the mechanics first and fit the fantasy around them, you'll end up with something that plays fine but doesn't sing.

Step 2

Identify the Classic Vulnerability

Every Folk Type has one — the thing that makes them dangerous to antagonize or expose. That vulnerability becomes the Limit. Choose the stress track based on what it actually threatens: Flesh if it wounds the body, Will if it attacks the supernatural self or severs the character's connection to their Folk nature, Mask if it strips away identity or social standing.

Silver threatens what a Werewolf is physically, so it lands on Flesh. The Revenant's disconnection from their corpse goes to Will. When in doubt, ask which attribute the Folk Type depends on most — then threaten that one.

Step 3

Build Three Active SFX

Spread coverage across different types of scenes. One SFX should support direct action — confrontation, combat, applying pressure. One should work in social or investigative territory. One should capture something distinctive to this Folk Type that the other two don't already cover. Don't write three variations on the same ability. If the core fantasy is strength, give them strength in one SFX and then find two other angles.

Step 4

Write the Plot Point Grant SFX

This is the most important SFX on the sheet. Find the moment of dramatic choice unique to this Folk Type — the thing a player might genuinely want to do, but that carries a real cost in context. The trigger has to mean something. If it's too easy to activate, players will farm it. If it never comes up, it doesn't exist.

Step 5

Write the Recovery SFX

Every Folk Type needs a way to heal that fits how they work. Vampires feed. The mechanic should feel like what this Folk Type actually does when they recover, not just a generic stress-step-down. This is often where the folk myth gives you the answer directly.

Step 6

Check Against the Design Principles

Every SFX should be something the player actively chooses to do — nothing passive. The Plot Point grant SFX should have genuine cost, not just a minor inconvenience. The Limit should follow standard structure. Anything that feels like a cantrip — minor, flavourful, low-stakes magic — doesn't need an SFX. Handle it narratively and save the mechanics for the moments that matter.
