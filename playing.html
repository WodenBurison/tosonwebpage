Chapter Two

Playing the Folk

Your character is a Folk, a monster living a human life, navigating two worlds simultaneously.

Attributes

Your character has three attributes. When you make a roll, you pick the one that best fits what you're doing and add it to your pool.

Attribute What It Covers
Flesh The body: strength, speed, endurance, physical action. Lifting something heavy, running, fighting, feats of supernatural strength or speed. If your body is doing the work, it's Flesh.
Will The mind: focus, clarity, emotional resistance, holding yourself together under pressure. Also your connection to your supernatural nature — magical ability, Folk-type powers that don't require physical action, resisting supernatural influence, maintaining control when your nature pushes against you.
Mask Social performance: passing as human, reading people, navigating human and Folk worlds alike. Folk networks, unofficial channels, the grey areas of the hidden world. The daily work of existing in a world that would destroy you if it knew what you were.

Roles

Roles describe what you do in the group — not a list of specific skills but a narrative function. When you make a roll, you choose the role that best fits the kind of thing you're doing. A character with a high Fang isn't just good at fighting. They're built for physical confrontation in all its forms.

Role Covers
The Fang Combat and physical confrontation. When violence is on the table, or when the threat of it is the point. Also intimidation through sheer physical presence — the kind that doesn't need words.
The Face Social navigation. Persuasion, negotiation, reading a room, working a connection, making the right impression on the right person. Also human-world social situations where maintaining your cover depends on being good with people.
The Shade Going unseen. Stealth, surveillance, tailing someone through a crowd, slipping in and out without leaving a trace.
The Tracker Investigation. Following a lead, finding a pattern in incomplete information, tracking someone through a city, uncovering what someone wanted to stay hidden.
The Ward Protection and support. Keeping others safe, providing backup, patching someone up when they're hurt. Being the steady presence the group relies on when things go wrong.
The Sage Knowledge. Folk history, supernatural expertise, identifying magic, knowing what a creature is and how it works. When something strange is happening and nobody understands it, the Sage has context.

Starting spread: d10, d8, d8, d6, d6, d4 (distributed across the six roles after completing Pathways). All Roles start at a minimum of d4.

Distinctions

Every character has three distinctions, each rated at d8. Where attributes and roles describe capability, distinctions describe identity: the specific, personal things that make your character who they are. A distinction isn't a single word. It's a short phrase that could only describe your character: not Vampire but Vampire Who Keeps Her Hunger Hidden, not Werewolf but Werewolf Running from a Pack That Won't Forget.

A good test for whether your distinctions are named well: you should be able to point to at least one of them on any given roll. If you can't, the name probably needs to be workshopped.

All three distinctions include the Hinder SFX automatically:

Hinder

Gain a Plot Point when you swap this distinction's d8 for a d4.

Hinder lets you turn your identity against yourself for a resource. Sometimes being what you are works against you. Hinder is how that pays off mechanically.

The Lineage

Your monster type. It carries all of your Folk Type's SFX. You start with two of your choice during Pathways and can unlock additional SFX through growth. Write it as a phrase that captures both your type and something specific about how you carry it: Fae Who Honors Every Word, Golem Still Learning What Hunger Means, Mage Who Knows the Cost of Everything.

The Root

Your background — where you came from and what made you. It carries only Hinder. Write it as a phrase that captures the formative thing: Raised in the Shadow of the Watch, Last of a Family the List Took, Grew Up Pretending the Human World Was Enough.

The Core

Your personality — who you choose to be and how you move through the world. Also Hinder only. Write it as a phrase that captures how you operate: I Protect What's Mine No Matter the Cost, The Smartest Person in the Room and Everyone Knows It, Peace Is Worth Whatever It Takes.

Hidden Nature

Every Folk character carries one Limit that isn't chosen and can't be traded away: Hidden Nature. It represents the constant pressure of living as something that humans aren't supposed to know exists.

Hidden Nature triggers when your true nature risks exposure: when you lose control, when circumstances push you toward revealing what you are, when a careless moment in the wrong place at the wrong time could unravel everything. It can come from how you're rolling, from what the scene demands, or from a choice you make knowing the cost.

The Plot Point is real compensation, but this isn't a resource to farm. Exposure doesn't just affect your character — it's a threat to the Peace that every Folk community depends on. The Watch exists precisely because individual moments of exposure become collective problems. Use the mechanic when the fiction calls for it, not to juice Plot Points from moments that wouldn't actually matter.

Limit: Hidden Nature

When your true nature risks exposure to unaware humans — through loss of control, a careless moment, or circumstances beyond your choosing — gain a Plot Point and the GM adds a d6 to the Scene Pool. If the exposure actually occurs, the GM may instead step up an existing die rather than adding a new one.

Stress & Trauma

When something hurts you — physically, mentally, or supernaturally — it becomes stress on one of your attributes. The GM or opponent chooses which attribute takes it, and the stress die sits there, added to the opposition roll every time you use that attribute. Getting hurt makes you worse at the thing that got hurt.

Stress escalates. If your stress die on an attribute ever exceeds that attribute's rating, you're Shaken: you can only keep one die for your total instead of two when using it. If you're already Shaken and another attribute takes stress beyond its rating — or if any single stress die steps up past d12 — you're Stricken: taken out of the scene until you've had time to recover.

When stress would step up past d12, it converts instead to Trauma, a d6 on that attribute that works the same way but is much harder to heal. Trauma that steps up past d12 means the character is gone for good. Dead, broken beyond recovery, or lost in a way that ends their story.

Stress can be recovered during a scene if someone helps or if you spend a Plot Point in the right moment. Trauma takes much more — at minimum, a full session. Stepping a trauma die down might take a whole story arc. It almost always requires help, and it should never feel easy.

Condition When Effect
Shaken Stress exceeds attribute rating Keep only 1 die for your total when using that attribute
Stricken Shaken + another attribute stressed, or stress past d12 Out of the scene until recovered
Trauma Stress steps past d12 d6 Trauma on that attribute; very hard to clear

Relationships

A Relationship is a bond with a specific person — someone your character knows, trusts, owes something to, fears, hates, or is tangled up with in a way that shows up when it matters. It's rated as a single die from d4 to d12. When that person is relevant to what you're doing (they're involved in the scene, they've influenced your situation, they're the reason you're doing this), add the die to your pool.

The die rating reflects how much that person means to you, not how much you like them. A d12 with the Watch enforcer hunting you is just as valid as a d12 with the person you'd die for.

Relationships start at d6 through Pathways and can be stepped up through Growth. If you don't have a Relationship for a given moment but the fiction calls for one, you can spend a Plot Point to create a temporary bond for the scene. It stays at d6 unless growth makes it permanent.

Resources

A Resource is something you have access to rather than a bond with: a location, an organization, a tool, funds, a contact you can call on. Resources are represented by two or more dice of the same size. When a resource is relevant, you choose how many of those dice to roll and add only the single highest result to your total. The dice you rolled are then spent.

Resources recover at the start of each session, after a meaningful period of downtime, or one for one by spending a Plot Point. The resource has to actually fit the situation, and if you're reaching, the GM will tell you.

Growth

Characters in TOSON grow through two mechanics: recovering with help from their allies, and completing goals they've set for themselves. Both feed into a growth pool — a pool of dice you accumulate over the course of a session and spend at the end in a tag scene. For full Growth Pool rules and tag scene structure, see CPGH.

Goals

Between sessions, each player can set up to three goals. Goals are specific and achievable, not vague aspirations but concrete situations: Find out who put Marcus Whitfield on the Watch List. Repair my relationship with the Folk council contact before the trial. Get off the Observed tier before the new year.

Goals are rated d6 to d12 based on difficulty. You can't assign the same rating to more than one goal. When you achieve a goal during play, mark it and add a die equal to its rating to your growth pool at the end of the session. Then set a new goal to replace it.

At any time you can surrender a goal: cross it off and add its die directly to your pool for one roll. It contributes nothing to growth.

Spending Growth

At the end of each session, run a brief tag scene — just enough narration to show how the session's events are settling into the character — then spend. Roll the growth pool against a difficulty the GM sets based on what you want to advance. Two best dice as your total. No effect die, no hitches, no Plot Points.

If you win, step up your trait or add a new one — the dice in the growth pool are spent. If you lose or tie, you can do it anyway, but you have to step something else down to compensate; the pool dice stick around for next session.

The Scene Pool

At the start of a scene, the GM describes what's happening and then sets the Scene Pool, anywhere from 3d6 to 6d12. The number of dice reflects how long or complex the scene is likely to be; the size of the dice reflects how dangerous.

When you make a roll, build your own pool first: pick your attribute, your role, your distinction if it applies, and any other relevant traits. Roll your pool. Choose any two dice as your total and one as your effect die. You're not locked into the highest.

Then the opposition rolls: the Scene Pool plus any GMC trait dice relevant to that moment. The two highest dice from that combined roll form the opposition total. GMC trait dice contribute to the roll but aren't part of the persistent pool — only Scene Pool dice can be targeted.

Compare totals. If yours beats the opposition, apply your effect die to whichever Scene Pool die you choose:

Effect die larger than the target → remove that die from the pool

Effect die equal to or smaller than the target → step down that die by one. A d4 stepped down is removed.

Hitches and Opportunities

Hitches: A 1 on any of your own dice is a hitch. The GM may respond in their next post by adding a die to the Scene Pool or stepping up an existing one.

Opportunities: A 1 on any opposition die is an opportunity. You may spend a Plot Point to remove it immediately.

Plot Points: Every player starts each session with at least 1 Plot Point.

The scene ends when the pool is cleared. Every player who participated earns a bonus Plot Point.

GMC Traits & Scale

When a GMC is present in a scene — a hunter, a Watch enforcer, a Folk elder with an agenda — they bring traits that add dice to the opposition roll alongside the Scene Pool. Those dice make the opposition total harder to beat, but they aren't part of the persistent pool. You can't target them with your effect die. Only Scene Pool dice get stepped down or removed.

Scale

Scale applies when you're dealing with a mismatch so extreme that one side has a fundamental structural advantage over the other — the kind of thing where it would never work for a normal person but a skilled character might just pull it off. A single Folk operative picking a fight with a Balance circle coordinating as an institution. A street-level character going up against the Van Helsing Society acting in its full capacity.

When scale matters, the GM adds a scale die — anywhere from d4 to d12, or split into multiple smaller dice — to the side with the advantage. That side also adds one extra die result to their total for free, the equivalent of spending a Plot Point. Regardless of how many scale dice are on the table, the advantaged side still only keeps one extra die in their total.

In practice, scale in TOSON usually comes up when a Faction is acting as an institution — the Watch issuing a formal response, the Society deploying resources as a coordinated operation. An individual Balance member following a lead doesn't bring scale with them; the circle mobilizing to correct someone does.
