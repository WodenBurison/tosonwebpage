Chapter One

Folk World

The Peace, the Watch, the Hollow, and the people who hunt.

The Folk

The Folk is the term monsters use among themselves for their hidden community. Not a place. A people. The Folk live and work alongside humans, unknown to most. Most Folk are born what they are. Turning happens, but it's rare. Most are Folk from birth, raised in the hidden world, taught its history and its rules alongside everything else.

Human Awareness

The human world is completely oblivious. Fringe theories exist — blogs, podcasts, the occasional book — but no credible mainstream voice believes them. The Folk are simply too good at the Peace for mainstream society to notice. Nobody believes people who claim to have seen something impossible. This is, by design, exactly how the Folk prefer it.

The Marker System

Every Folk has a unique magical signature, a marker that identifies their type. Humans have their own marker. Mages, for example, carry a distinct Mage marker rather than a human one — they appear human but read correctly to magic. This system underpins the Hollow's access enchantments and has broader implications for how Folk identify each other in person.

The Hollow

The Folk have always found ways to communicate. The Hollow is simply the latest and most elegant solution.

The Hollow is a magical internet built on a programming language developed by Mages and maintained by independent Tech Mages worldwide who build and tinker with it for the love of the craft. It is largely self-sustaining by design: no single controlling authority, no central server, no kill switch. It exists because the Folk needed it and Mages built it.

Access

Every Folk has a unique magical marker, a signature that identifies them as something other than human. Tech Mages — a specialization focused on the intersection of magic and modern technology — can enchant any personal device to read its user's marker. A human picking up an enchanted device gets nothing. The enchantment checks the person, not the hardware.

A human who stumbles onto a Hollow URL is redirected to folk.net, a mundane, well-maintained folklore and mythology website that raises no flags. Folk are taken directly to the Hollow's front page, a search engine that serves as the gateway to everything built on the network.

Tech Mages are common enough in cities that most Folk can find one without difficulty. The Hollow's signal is effectively perfect everywhere on Earth. Access is limited only by proximity to Tech Mages and Magetech, not infrastructure. Any Folk with the inclination can learn the Hollow's programming language and build their own site.

Major Sites

Kindred is the Folk social network, the place where communities form, news spreads, and the hidden world keeps in touch across cities and countries.

The Exchange is the Folk marketplace, built around the Favor currency system. Folk have traded in Favor for centuries. An ancient ritual called a Pact bestows the right to a favor, creating currency that old families may still carry as physical coins while most Folk now use a Magetech digital equivalent. The Exchange runs on both.

The Burrow is the Folk forum: unfiltered, opinionated, and occasionally chaotic. News, rumors, debates about the Watch, complaints about hunter activity, and threads that have been running for decades. If something is happening in the Folk world, someone in the Burrow knows about it.

The Watch on the Hollow

The Watch maintains an official presence: a dedicated site for List updates, trial announcements, and council communications. The List is maintained by a team of Folk administrators selected for their impartiality. No supernatural mandate, no special ability. Just people who take the responsibility seriously and have earned the community's trust.

Dark Corners

The Hollow's open architecture means nobody controls what gets built on it. Sites and apps exist that the Watch would prefer didn't. What lives in those corners is left to the Folk who find them, and to the GMs and players who choose to go looking.

The Watch

The Watch is the governing body that maintains the Watch List and adjudicates trials: a council of 100 members, split equally between humans and Folk. Day-to-day administration is handled by a permanent staff drawn from both sides. When a decision is needed, members are selected by random draw, typically 9 per case — an odd number to prevent ties. Members drawn for one case are unavailable for simultaneous cases until their duty is complete. Multiple crises at once strains the council's resources, a fact that savvy players may exploit.

Every council member has their own agenda. The Watch has no unified institutional goal beyond maintaining the List. This makes the draw high-stakes. Who gets pulled matters enormously.

The Watch List

A list maintained by the Watch and shared with hunter organizations. Folk who disturb the Peace lose their protection in degrees.

Council Term Folk Term Status
Observed Shadowed Flagged for monitoring. No action yet.
Listed Marked No harassment protection. Hunters may engage if provoked. Some hunter orgs deliberately provoke Marked Folk.
Condemned Gone Active hunt authorized. No protection remaining.

Getting on the List is easy. Getting off is not.

The Trial System

A Listed Folk may petition for a trial, but must first find a Sponsor — a council member willing to formally request a hearing. No Sponsor, no trial. Finding one is itself an adventure; council members don't take the risk for nothing.

In the trial, the burden of proof falls on the Listed Folk, not the council. They must demonstrate the incident never occurred, that circumstances justified their actions, or that they have meaningfully changed. The council votes. Removal from the List is rare. A Condemned Folk has never been successfully cleared, though no one can prove the vote is rigged.

Before the Peace

The Folk have always lived alongside humanity, hidden in plain sight. For most of recorded history that arrangement was enforced not by agreement but by necessity: humans who discovered the Folk hunted them, and the Folk kept their existence secret to survive. There was no Peace. There was only the quiet, ongoing war of concealment.

The organizations that hunted Folk were not unified. They were family lines, religious orders, private societies, each operating independently, each carrying their own traditions and their own grudges. Some were methodical. Some were zealous. All of them understood that the Folk were real and that most humans would never believe them.

The Folk, for their part, had no formal relationship with the hunters beyond survival. You avoided them when you could. You ran when you had to. You fought when there was no other choice. The idea of sitting across a table from a hunter and reaching an understanding was not something most Folk considered possible.

The Second World War changed that.

The War

When the war came the Folk were not separate from it. They were inside it, as they had always been inside human history: present, invisible, shaped by the same forces that shaped everyone else. Folk followed their convictions the way humans did. Vampires, Werewolves, Fae, Mages, Revenants, and Golems fought on both sides of the conflict. Those aligned with the Axis believed in what the Axis promised or feared what defiance would cost. Those aligned with the Allies understood with the particular clarity of people who had survived centuries of persecution what a world shaped by Axis ideology would mean — not just for humans but for every Folk who had ever hidden what they were.

Allied Folk fought. Not always openly. Not always in ways that appeared in any human record. But they fought.

The hunters were also inside the war. The old organizations — the family lines, the private societies — had members on the front lines, in intelligence services, in resistance movements. Some of them began to notice things. Impossible things. A soldier who should have died from his wounds who was back on his feet the next morning. A resistance operative who moved through occupied territory with uncanny ease. A Mage whose ritual work turned the tide of an action that had no business succeeding.

The hunters noticed. And eventually, quietly, some of them made a choice that would have been unthinkable before the war. They reached out.

The Emergence of the Peace

It was not a summit. There was no document signed. No single moment when humanity and the Folk shook hands and agreed to coexist. It was conversations: careful, suspicious, conducted in the margins of a war that was consuming everything. A hunter who needed something only a Vampire could provide. A Folk who needed cover only a hunter's network could offer. Grudging cooperation that became, over months and years of shared danger and shared loss, something closer to respect.

When the war ended the participants carried that experience home with them. The idea that had seemed impossible — that hunters and Folk might simply leave each other alone — had been tested in the worst conditions imaginable and had not broken. It spread. Not quickly. Not cleanly. But it spread.

The Folk began to call it the Peace. The hunter organizations, when they needed to put a name to it in their internal documents, called it the Accord. No one called it finished. Some hunters refused it entirely, and their refusal was the seed of the Watch List — a compromise that acknowledged the Peace while preserving the right to act against Folk who genuinely caused harm.

The Peace has no birthday. It has no founding document. What it has is the weight of lived experience: the memory, held by Folk old enough to remember, of what the world looked like before it. Vampires and Fae who fought in that war are still alive. Some of them sit on the Watch council. Some of them teach.

In Folk schools the war and the emergence of the Peace are required history. Young Folk learn what their elders paid for the world they inherited. They learn the names of hunters who chose differently. They learn that the Peace is not a gift but an argument that has to be made again, every day, by every Folk who chooses to live quietly alongside the humans who would never believe they existed.

The argument is made most powerfully by simply being good neighbors.

The Van Helsing Society

"We were here before the Peace. We will be here after."
A private global security company with ancient roots and older grudges. To the world they are a legitimate enterprise. To the Folk they are a reminder that some wounds never heal.

Origin

The Society traces its roots to a collection of old European families who hunted Folk long before the Peace — nobility, landowners, and warriors who passed knowledge of the hidden world down through bloodlines across centuries. The Van Helsing name became the banner under which these scattered families united. When the Peace came they disagreed with it loudly, then quietly, then not at all. Publicly.

Attitude Toward the Peace

Hostile. The Society views the Peace as a political mistake made by people who had forgotten what the Folk truly are. They comply with its terms precisely because violation would expose them. They are waiting.

Structure

The Society is built on family lines — old European bloodlines who have hunted for generations and new lines founded when someone has been severely wronged by the Folk. A traumatized survivor may be approached, vetted, and brought into the fold. Their grief becomes training. Their anger becomes purpose. Their children are born into it.

Above the family lines sits a private council of senior family heads: the Board in public documents, the Table in internal communication.

Day to Day

Publicly the Society operates as Vanguard Global Security — a legitimate private security firm providing protection services, threat assessment, and crisis management worldwide. It is genuinely profitable. The cover is real. Members have careers, titles, business cards. Underneath, they train, research, and monitor. Every regional office maintains a file on local Folk.

Resources

Centuries of accumulated knowledge: bestiary records, hunting techniques, Folk weaknesses, historical accounts. A global network of legitimate business contacts. Weapons and equipment developed specifically for hunting Folk. Old money, new revenue.

Relationship to the Watch

The Society does not officially participate in the Watch. They view it as a Folk-sympathetic institution. However, some Society-affiliated humans have obtained council seats through legitimate channels — a fact the Society neither confirms nor denies.

Adventure Hooks

A new family line is being established in the players' city — someone local was hurt badly by Folk. The Society is already there.

A Vanguard Global Security contract brings Society operatives into proximity with the players' lives through entirely legitimate business. Someone is watching.

A senior Society family member reaches out quietly — they want to defect. They know things. The Society knows they're talking.

The Balance

"We do not hate. We correct."
A spiritual philosophy of cosmic equilibrium, practiced quietly by those whose eyes have been opened to the hidden world. They are not hunters by nature. They simply believe some things need removing.

Origin

The Balance emerged in the late twentieth century from overlapping communities of people who had encountered the Folk and were looking for a framework to make sense of what they had seen. No founder, no scripture, no moment of revelation. Just a spiritual philosophy that spread through conversation, small gatherings, and quiet recognition. The core belief is simple: the universe tends toward balance, and beings who cause harm without consequence are a disruption of that balance. Removing them is not violence. It is correction.

Attitude Toward the Peace

Compliant. The Balance respects the Peace genuinely — they view it as a reasonable framework for identifying which Folk are disruptive. The Watch List is their guide. They do not act on Observed Folk. They monitor Marked Folk. They act on Condemned Folk when they determine it is appropriate. They have never been brought before the Watch for a violation. This is not an accident.

Structure

The Balance has no hierarchy in the traditional sense. Local communities called circles gather regularly for discussion, meditation, and mutual support. Recruitment is handled through personal relationships — members extend quiet invitations to humans whose eyes have been opened. Nothing is rushed. Nothing is forced.

Day to Day

Members of the Balance live ordinary lives. They are teachers, doctors, social workers, artists. The philosophy is kept private — not secret exactly, but personal. They do not proselytize to humans who haven't already seen the hidden world. Correction — their term for hunting — is approached with solemnity. It is not celebrated.

Resources

The Balance's greatest resource is people — a broad network of ordinary humans in ordinary jobs who see everything and raise no flags. Medical professionals, lawyers, researchers, academics. They are not wealthy as an organization but their members collectively have reach that money can't easily buy.

Relationship to the Watch

The Balance follows the Watch List carefully and considers it the legitimate authority on which Folk have broken the Peace. Some members have joined the Watch council as private citizens — their affiliation with the Balance is not publicly known.

Adventure Hooks

A player character's human friend or family member has recently joined a Balance circle. They seem calmer, more purposeful. They ask careful questions.

A Condemned Folk in the players' city has been cleanly removed. No evidence, no noise, no leads. The Watch is asking questions nobody wants answered.

A Balance circle has begun quietly monitoring a player character they believe may be moving toward the List. They haven't acted. Yet.
